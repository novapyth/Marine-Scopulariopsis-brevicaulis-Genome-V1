### *Scopulariopsis brevicaulis* LF580

The marine-derived fungus *Scopulariopsis brevicaulis* LF580 is a promising source of bioactive secondary metabolites, particularly scopularides A and B, cyclic lipopeptides that exhibit anticancer activity. Despite its biotechnological potential, limited genomic information has been available regarding the genetic basis underlying the biosynthesis of these compounds and the ecological adaptation of this species to marine environments. To address these questions, we performed comprehensive genome sequencing and functional genomic analyses of *S. brevicaulis* LF580.

A high-quality draft genome was generated using a combination of three next-generation sequencing technologies followed by a de novo hybrid assembly approach. The resulting genome assembly comprised 621 scaffolds with a total genome size of approximately 32.2 Mb. Genome annotation predicted 16,298 putative protein-coding genes, providing a detailed genetic framework for investigating the metabolic capabilities and ecological characteristics of this fungus.

Particular attention was given to the identification of biosynthetic pathways responsible for scopularide production. Genome mining revealed a large non-ribosomal peptide synthetase gene, designated **nrps1**, together with a supporting polyketide synthase gene, **pks2**, located within the same biosynthetic gene cluster. These genes are believed to play a central role in the biosynthesis of scopularides. Transcriptomic analysis using RNA sequencing confirmed that both the biosynthetic cluster and its constituent genes are actively expressed, providing functional evidence for their involvement in secondary metabolite production.

In addition to secondary metabolism, the genome was analysed to investigate the ecological adaptation and evolutionary origin of *S. brevicaulis* LF580. Comprehensive examination of carbohydrate-active enzymes (CAZymes) and major facilitator superfamily (MFS) transporters revealed genomic characteristics typically associated with terrestrial fungi. These findings support the hypothesis that *S. brevicaulis* LF580 originated as a soil-dwelling fungus and subsequently established an ecological association with the marine sponge *Tethya aurantium*.

The marine sponge likely serves as a protective habitat, providing favourable environmental conditions and nutritional resources that enable the fungus to survive and persist within marine ecosystems. This symbiotic or commensal relationship may have contributed to the retention and diversification of its secondary metabolic capabilities.

Overall, the genome sequence of *S. brevicaulis* LF580 provides important insights into both the biosynthesis of anticancer compounds and the ecological adaptation of marine-associated fungi. The identification of an actively expressed scopularide biosynthetic gene cluster establishes a foundation for future studies aimed at pathway engineering, natural product discovery, and biotechnological exploitation. Furthermore, the genomic resources generated in this study offer valuable opportunities for investigating the relationship between fungal lifestyle, host association, and secondary metabolite production.
